Written Article · Youth & Children's Education
Teaching Islam to Children: Nurturing Faith from the Earliest Years
+
An educational article exploring evidence-based, Islamically-grounded approaches to teaching young children about their faith — from aqeedah fundamentals to Quranic habits — drawing on prophetic guidance and contemporary pedagogy.
Overview

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Every child is born in a state of fitrah" (Sahih al-Bukhari). This foundational truth shapes the entire philosophy of Islamic early childhood education: children are not blank slates to be filled, but souls already inclined toward their Creator — and our role as educators is to nurture, not impose.

"Command your children to pray when they are seven years old, and discipline them for it when they are ten."
— Prophet Muhammad ﷺ · Sunan Abu Dawud, 495
Key Themes Covered

1. Age-Appropriate Aqeedah: Introducing the concept of Allah ﷻ to young children through His beautiful names (Al-Rahman, Al-Wadud, Al-Khaliq) rather than abstract theological definitions. Stories of creation, gratitude, and wonder form the foundation.

2. The Role of Routine and Ritual: How daily Islamic practices — saying Bismillah before meals, making du'a before sleep, hearing Quran during play — become anchors of faith identity long before formal instruction begins. Research in child development confirms that ritual repetition builds both neural pathways and emotional security.

3. Storytelling as Pedagogy: The Quran itself uses narrative as its primary teaching tool. Drawing on the stories of the Prophets (Anbiya), particularly those appropriate for young children — Prophet Yunus, Prophet Ibrahim, and Prophet Isa — to teach values of patience, trust in Allah, and courage.

4. The Educator's Intention (Niyyah): Grounding the teaching relationship in sincerity. Ibn Khaldun's Muqaddimah notes that the teacher's character is the most powerful curriculum. Children absorb the iman of those around them more than any lesson plan.

Curriculum Recommendations

The article concludes with a practical framework for weekend Islamic school programs (Ages 5–10), including a sample weekly structure: Quran recitation warm-up (10 min) → Themed story with discussion (15 min) → Craft or activity reinforcing the lesson (20 min) → Du'a and closing reflection (5 min). This structure reflects the Prophet's ﷺ practice of varied, engaging, and never burdensome teaching.

Long-Form Article · Islamic Scholarship
The Etiquettes of Seeking Knowledge in Islam
+
A 2,800-word article examining adab al-talab al-'ilm — the proper etiquettes of seeking Islamic knowledge — through the lens of classical scholars. Written for adult Muslim readers navigating the balance between traditional learning and contemporary life.
Introduction

Imam al-Shafi'i once said: "Knowledge is not what is memorised — knowledge is what benefits." In an age of information abundance, the Islamic tradition's emphasis on the adab (etiquette) of learning is more urgent than ever. Classical scholars did not merely transmit information; they transmitted a way of being with knowledge — a posture of humility, sincerity, and reverence that transformed the student before transforming the world.

"Seek knowledge even if it be in China."
— Attributed to the Prophet ﷺ · Cited in classical works on the pursuit of knowledge
The Classical Framework

Sincerity of Intention (Ikhlas): Imam al-Nawawi opens his Majmu' with a reminder that knowledge sought for worldly gain loses its barakah. The student must constantly renew the intention that their learning is for the sake of Allah ﷻ and the benefit of the ummah — not status or admiration.

Honouring the Teacher: Ibn Jama'ah's Tadhkirat al-Sami' dedicates entire chapters to the student-teacher relationship. The student should not contradict the teacher publicly, should arrive early and leave last, and should make du'a for their teacher's wellbeing. This is not blind deference — it is the recognition that knowledge is transmitted through hearts, not merely texts.

Acting Upon What is Learned: Al-Khatib al-Baghdadi's Iqtida al-'Ilm al-'Amal (Knowledge Demands Action) is perhaps the most direct classical treatment of this principle. Knowledge that does not change one's character and behaviour is considered by the scholars to be a liability, not an asset.

"The scholar whose knowledge is not acted upon is like a lamp that gives light to others while burning itself."
— Classical Arabic proverb cited in adab al-'ilm literature
Contemporary Application

The article concludes by examining how these classical etiquettes apply to modern Islamic education in Canada — from online learning platforms to weekend schools. The challenge of the digital age is not access to knowledge, but the cultivation of the inner qualities that make knowledge transformative. Programs like those at ISNA Canada that combine structured Islamic education with mentorship and community are the closest contemporary equivalent to the classical halaqah model.

Project Proposal · Digital Content Series
Ramadan Reflection Series — 30-Day Social Content Plan
+
A proposed 30-day Islamically-researched social content series designed for a Canadian Muslim community platform. The concept centres on daily posts pairing a Quranic ayah, hadith, or scholarly reflection with a short, meaningful prompt to help community members stay spiritually engaged throughout Ramadan.
The Vision

Ramadan is the month of the Quran — yet for many community members, the spiritual potential of the month can get lost in routine. This project proposes a structured 30-day content series that meets people where they already are (social media) and draws them closer to the Quran, the Sunnah, and intentional reflection — one post at a time.

The series would follow the classical tripartite division of Ramadan: the first ten days themed around Mercy (Rahmah), the middle ten around Forgiveness (Maghfirah), and the final ten around Freedom from the Fire (Itq min al-Nar) — giving the month a meaningful narrative arc rather than a collection of disconnected posts.

Proposed Content Structure (Sample Days)
Day 1 · Mercy — Concept
Open with Quran 2:185 and the hadith on the gates of Jannah. Prompt: "Name one mercy of Allah you often overlook. Write it down today." Designed to set a tone of gratitude from day one.
Day 14 · Forgiveness — Concept
Draw on Ibn al-Qayyim's writings on the heart. Prompt: "Is there someone you need to forgive — not for them, but to free yourself?" Paired with du'a for a softened heart.
Day 27 · Laylat al-Qadr — Concept
Reference Quran 97:1–5 and the Prophet's ﷺ increased worship in the last ten nights. Prompt: "Write your du'a list — not just what you want, but who you are praying for."
Research & Quality Standards

Every post in the proposed series would be grounded in verified sources. All Quranic references would be drawn from established tafsir works (Ibn Kathir, al-Sa'di, and Tafhim al-Quran). All hadith would be checked for authenticity via Sunnah.com and cross-referenced before publication — ensuring the community only receives sound, reliable Islamic content.

Visually, each post would follow a consistent branded template (emerald, gold, and white) designed in Canva, optimised for both Instagram and Facebook. A content scheduling tool would ensure daily posting consistency throughout the month without last-minute production pressure.

Why This Matters

Islamic social media content is often either too shallow to be spiritually meaningful or too academic to be accessible. This series is designed to bridge that gap — rooted in scholarship, but written in a voice that speaks to everyday Canadian Muslims: students, parents, professionals, and new Muslims alike. The goal is not viral content — it is content that plants seeds of taqwa.

Sources & References

Familiarity with Islamic sources is not simply a list of titles — it is an ongoing relationship with scholarship. Below are key works I have engaged with, and how each has shaped my approach to Islamic education and content development.

Classical · Tafsir
Tafsir Ibn Kathir (Tafsir al-Quran al-Azim)
Imam Ibn Kathir al-Dimashqi (d. 1373 CE)
My primary reference for Quranic content development. I rely on Ibn Kathir to verify the context (asbab al-nuzul) of ayat before using them in educational material, ensuring nothing is quoted out of its intended meaning. His narrative style also informs how I present Quranic stories to younger audiences.
Classical · Tafsir
Tafsir al-Sa'di (Taysir al-Karim al-Rahman)
Shaykh Abdur Rahman al-Sa'di (d. 1956 CE)
I turn to al-Sa'di when writing for general audiences — his tafsir is concise, accessible, and rich in practical lessons. For social media content and community-facing writing, his explanations translate well into modern reflective prompts without losing scholarly grounding.
Classical · Spirituality
Ihya 'Ulum al-Din (Revival of the Religious Sciences)
Imam Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (d. 1111 CE)
Al-Ghazali's Ihya has been foundational to understanding how Islamic knowledge should transform the inner self — not just inform the mind. This shapes how I design educational content: I ask not only "what will students learn?" but "how will this change how they feel and act?" His sections on sincerity and intention directly informed my article on the etiquettes of seeking knowledge.
Classical · Adab al-'Ilm
Tadhkirat al-Sami' wal-Mutakallim
Ibn Jama'ah al-Kinani (d. 1333 CE)
This is perhaps the most detailed classical manual on the etiquettes of the student-teacher relationship. I studied it while researching my article on adab al-talab al-'ilm — particularly its chapters on how the student should carry themselves in the presence of a teacher. It has changed how I approach my own role as both a learner and an educator.
Contemporary · Seerah
The Sealed Nectar (Al-Rahiq al-Makhtum)
Shaykh Safiur Rahman al-Mubarakpuri (Darussalam, 1979)
My go-to reference for seerah content aimed at youth. Al-Mubarakpuri's narrative is detailed yet flowing, making it well-suited for extracting stories and lessons that resonate with younger learners. I use it as the backbone for any Prophet ﷺ-focused curriculum I develop, cross-referencing with Ibn Hisham for primary sourcing.
Contemporary · Islamic Education
Being Muslim: A Practical Guide
Asad Tarsin (Sandala Inc., 2015)
One of the clearest contemporary introductions to Islamic practice written for Western Muslim audiences. I draw on its structure and tone when developing beginner-level Islamic content — it demonstrates how classical fiqh rulings can be explained accessibly without sacrificing accuracy. A constant reference when designing content for new Muslims or younger students.
Contemporary · Community & Education
Agenda to Change Our Condition
Hamza Yusuf & Zaid Shakir (Zaytuna Institute, 2007)
This short but powerful work frames Islamic education as an act of community renewal. It has shaped my understanding of why Islamic education in Canada matters beyond individual piety — it is about building a generation of Muslims who are grounded, contributing, and spiritually resilient. I return to it when I need to reconnect with the purpose behind the work.
Research Tool · Hadith Sciences
Sunnah.com — Authenticated Hadith Database
Collections: Bukhari, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Nasai, Ibn Majah
My primary tool for hadith research and verification. Before any hadith appears in content I produce — whether a social media post, lesson plan, or article — I verify its chain and grading through Sunnah.com, cross-referencing with Shaykh al-Albani's gradings where applicable. Ensuring the soundness of every narration is non-negotiable.
